Some scholars consider articles to be a type of adjective, or in the same category with adjectives. Adjectives are words that describe a noun. Articles are similar to adjectives in that they modify nouns. However, unlike adjectives, they do not actually describe a noun; they just identify it. So some English language experts categorize articles as their own part of speech, separate from adjectives. For the purposes of this lesson, articles will be considered their own part of speech.
But these three little words are not interchangeable. They are crucial when referring to nouns in the English language, and each one is used in specific ways and in particular contexts.
"The" is the only definite article in English. When the article "the" is used to precede a word beginning with a consonant, it's pronounced "the" with a short "e" (also called a schwa) sound. The schwa sound is represented by an upside-down e symbol, usually within forward slashes.
